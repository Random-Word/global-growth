# Measuring global poverty: where both sides get it
# right and wrong
The empirical record on global poverty is genuinely better than Jason Hickel claims
and genuinely worse than the optimist narrative suggests. Both sides of this heated
debate make legitimate methodological points grounded in real scholarship — and both
sides make rhetorical moves that exceed their evidence. The World Bank’s $1.90/day line is
indeed too low to capture meaningful deprivation, a point acknowledged by virtually
everyone including the Bank itself. But poverty rates have declined at every measurable
threshold, non-income welfare indicators confirm massive improvements in human well-
being since 1981, and Hickel’s alternative metrics, while methodologically interesting,
produce some results (particularly on Maoist China) that strain credibility against other
evidence. The deepest insight from this debate is not that one side is right, but that
measuring human welfare across vastly different economies and time periods is fiendishly
difficult — and the choice of methodology is never ideologically innocent.

The empirical rebuttals are powerful but not complete
The mainstream response, articulated most thoroughly by Max Roser and Joe Hasell at Our
World in Data, Charles Kenny at the Center for Global Development, Noah Smith, and
various development economists, rests on several strong empirical pillars.
The single most devastating counterargument is distributional: poverty rates have
declined at every measurable income threshold. Kenny documented that at $1.90, the
rate fell from 35% to 11% between 1990 and 2013; at $7.40, from 87% to 68%; at $10, from
92% to 77%. Even at $100/day, the 2013 figure was fractionally lower than 1990. cgdev  As
Smith emphasizes, the entire global income distribution has shifted rightward — this is not
merely an artifact of people crossing a single arbitrary line. Chen and Ravallion’s research
confirmed this pattern for every threshold from $1 to $13 per day between 1981 and 2008.
Wikipedia
Critics argue persuasively that the rising absolute number of people below $7.40/day is
primarily a population growth story, not an impoverishment story. Kenny states it plainly:
“The number can increase because people are getting poorer or because poor people are

living longer and having children that survive. And as it turns out, the big, overwhelming
reason there are more poor people is because people are dying less, not that they are
earning less.” Center For Global Development  cgdev  The world population grew from 4.5
billion to 7.3 billion between 1981 and 2015. Framing the rising headcount as evidence that
progress is illusory conflates demographic success with economic failure.
On the China question, Roser shows that while China drove a disproportionate share of
poverty reduction (contributing roughly 75% of global reduction at $1.90 from 1990 to
2010), ScienceDirect  excluding China still shows the poverty rate falling from 33% to 12%
between 1990 and 2025. India, Bangladesh, Vietnam, Indonesia, and Ethiopia have all
achieved substantial poverty reduction. Wikipedia  Smith adds the obvious point: “If you
want to assess the change in global poverty in an ideologically neutral way, why on Earth
would you exclude China? China is a fifth of the entire human race.”
Perhaps most importantly, non-income welfare indicators independently confirm
massive improvement. Global life expectancy rose from 64 to 71 years between 1990 and
2021. Under-five child mortality fell 59%, from 93 to 37 per 1,000 live births. Daily caloric
supply in developing countries increased by 400–600 kcal per person. Safe water access
rose from 62% to 74% (safely managed, 2000–2020). Literacy climbed from roughly 76% to
87%. These improvements are not artifacts of PPP methodology or poverty line placement
— they are measured directly.
The PPP methodology debate reveals genuine uncertainty and
genuine overstatement
The technical dispute over Purchasing Power Parity adjustments illustrates how both sides
mix legitimate concerns with rhetorical excess. Hickel cites Sanjay Reddy and Thomas
Pogge’s influential critique “How Not to Count the Poor,” which identified three fundamental
flaws: the poverty line is detached from human needs, the PPP methodology was designed
for comparing national economies rather than measuring whether poor people can afford
basic necessities, and the apparent precision masks enormous uncertainty.
Semantic Scholar  These concerns are shared, to varying degrees, by Nobel laureate Angus
Deaton, who noted that “PPP comparisons between widely different countries rest on weak
theoretical and empirical foundations” IDEAS/RePEc  and that shifting the poverty line by
just 10 cents could change the global poverty count by 100 million people.
Academia.edu
However, Hickel’s specific claim that PPP baskets are distorted by including luxury items like
“commercial airfares, sports cars, and meals at high-end restaurants” is rebutted by the
actual ICP methodology. Tim Worstall documented from World Bank data that PPP
calculations weight items by actual expenditure share in each economy. If eating out

constitutes 0% of expenditure in a subsistence economy, it receives 0% weight. Substack
This does not eliminate all concerns — Reddy and Pogge’s point that the poor spend
disproportionately on food staples compared to the average consumption basket remains
valid — but Hickel’s framing significantly overstates the distortion.

from failures of the law of one price, creating real but bounded uncertainty. The aggregate
impact on global poverty counts was moderate, not catastrophic. Robert Allen’s alternative
BNPL approach — which avoids PPP conversions entirely by using local prices of specific

magnitude difference Hickel’s rhetoric sometimes implies.
The China BNPL results are the weakest link in Hickel’s chain
Hickel’s most extraordinary empirical claim — published with Sullivan and Moatsos in New
Political Economy — is that using the BNPL methodology, China under socialism had only
5.6% extreme poverty from 1981 to 1990, compared to 51% in India, 36.5% in Indonesia,
and 29.5% in Jussemper  Brazil. Substack  Jussemper  The BNPL further shows Chinese
poverty allegedly increasing to a peak of 68% in 1995 during market reforms, before
gradually recovering. ScienceDirect +2
This finding strains credulity against multiple independent lines of evidence. The Great Leap
Forward (1959–1961) killed an estimated 15–55 million people through famine — the worst
famine in human history. Chinese life expectancy in the early 1980s, while improved from
the worst Maoist periods, still reflected decades of severe deprivation. As Worstall
observed, “It doesn’t really matter what the price of a bowl of rice was in China if there were
no bowls — at those low, low, socialist prices — of rice to be had.” Substack  The BNPL
methodology measures whether incomes can afford basic necessities at prevailing prices,
but under price controls with severe shortages, low nominal prices do not translate to actual
access. The same methodological sensitivity Hickel criticizes in PPP adjustments — that
monetary measures can fail to capture real welfare — applies to his own BNPL when price
controls mask scarcity.

For the 200-year view, the evidence confirms that material welfare has improved
enormously and unambiguously — life expectancy more than doubled, Our World in Data
child mortality fell by over 90%, literacy rose from 12% to 87%, Statista  caloric availability
increased substantially. The precise trajectory of income poverty before 1981, especially
during the colonial period, remains legitimately uncertain. Both the optimist claim of
smooth, continuous progress and the critical claim of net immiseration through colonialism
outrun the available data. The most defensible reading is that the colonial period involved
genuine dispossession and suffering alongside eventual long-run material improvements —
improvements that accelerated dramatically only in the mid-twentieth century, coinciding
with decolonization, public health advances, and the Green Revolution.
Conclusion: measurement choices carry moral weight

This debate ultimately reveals that poverty measurement is not a purely technical exercise
— it is a political act with material consequences. Setting the line at $1.90 makes the world
look like a success story and reduces pressure for redistribution. Setting it at $7.40 makes
the world look like a catastrophe and strengthens the case for structural transformation.
Both lines describe the same underlying reality; they differ in what they ask us to care
about.
The most important empirical insight both sides share — confirmed in the Hickel-Kenny
consensus — is that at any threshold, poverty rates have declined since 1990,
Center For Global Development  but that this progress has been far more dramatic at the
bottom of the distribution than in the middle. Center For Global Development  The world has
become considerably better at preventing the very worst deprivation while making only
modest progress on broader material security. Some 3.5 billion people remain below the
upper-middle-income poverty threshold, and this number has barely moved in three
decades. New Internationalist  Meanwhile, the world economy has more than enough
productive capacity to eliminate this deprivation entirely New Internationalist  Substack  — a
point on which both Hickel and mainstream economists broadly agree. The disagreement is
about whether this requires systemic transformation or continued growth. That is not a
question empirical methodology alone can answer.
