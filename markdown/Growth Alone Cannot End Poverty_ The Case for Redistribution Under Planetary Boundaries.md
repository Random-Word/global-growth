# Growth alone cannot end poverty at meaningful
# thresholds
Eliminating poverty at higher, more realistic poverty lines ($6.85–$10/day) through
economic growth alone would require multiplying global GDP by 5 to 175 times its
current size — a process taking one to two centuries that is flatly incompatible with
planetary survival. This is the central finding across a convergent body of research from
ecological economists, the World Bank itself, and empirical studies of the growth-poverty
relationship. The arithmetic is devastating: only about 5% of all new income from GDP
growth reaches the poorest 60% of humanity, Giving What We Can +4  making growth-
based poverty elimination grotesquely inefficient. Meanwhile, the same poverty goals could
be achieved through redistribution of roughly $1.3–6 trillion per year — a fraction of the
richest 1%’s annual income New Internationalist  — with negligible ecological cost. The
question of whether to grow or redistribute is no longer academic; it is a question with a
calculable answer.

Growth becomes less effective as the poverty bar rises
The “growth elasticity of poverty” (GEP) — the percentage reduction in poverty for each 1%
increase in mean income — is well-documented to decline sharply at higher poverty lines.
This is the empirical mechanism behind Woodward’s projections.
At the $1.90/day extreme poverty line, the consensus GEP ranges from -2.0 to -3.0: a 1%
increase in mean household consumption reduces extreme poverty by 2–3%. ScienceDirect
A major 2024 World Bank study using 575 growth spells (1981–2021) confirmed a GEP of
approximately -2.0 relative to survey means and -2.8 relative to GDP per capita. worldbank
World Bank  But at the $2/day line, Ram (2011) found the global GEP collapses to just -0.84
— less than half the extreme-poverty elasticity. For South Asia at $2/day, it was -0.22; for

India, a mere -0.13. Taylor & Francis Online  IDEAS/RePEc  At thresholds of $5.50 or $6.85/day,
the elasticity falls even further toward zero, though fewer cross-country regression
estimates exist at these exact lines.
The reasons are both mechanical and structural. When the poverty rate is high (as it is at
higher thresholds — 44% at $6.85/day versus 8.5% at $2.15/day), World Bank  World Bank
a given reduction in the number of poor translates to a smaller proportional decline in the
rate. UNU-WIDER  Econstor  More importantly, growth benefits concentrate among those
nearest the poverty line, leaving the deeply poor behind. Bourguignon’s foundational 2003
framework showed that the elasticity is an increasing function of the ratio of mean income
to the poverty line and a decreasing function of inequality. UNU WIDER +2  Sub-Saharan
Africa, where poverty is most concentrated, has a GEP roughly half the global average
ResearchGate  (-0.9 to -1.3 versus -2.0 to -3.0), meaning growth there is structurally less
poverty-reducing. worldbank  ScienceDirect  A 2024 study of 45 African countries found
that economic growth begins to reduce extreme poverty only when inequality (Gini) falls
below specific thresholds — 35.28 for low-income and 45.15 for middle-income countries
— and has no relationship to poverty above those inequality levels. ScienceDirect
An important nuance: the 2024 World Bank study found that the GEP has not declined over

growth, not a weakening elasticity. World Bank  World Bank Blogs  However, poverty is now
geographically concentrated in regions (Sub-Saharan Africa, fragile states) where the
elasticity was always low, World Bank  World Bank  producing the same practical effect.
Hickel and Sullivan reframe the question entirely
Jason Hickel and Dylan Sullivan’s 2024 paper in World Development Perspectives — “How
much growth is required to achieve good lives for all?” — inverts the standard growth
calculus. Rather than asking how much GDP is needed, they calculate the physical
resources required to provide decent living standards (DLS) for 8.5 billion people:
ScienceDirect  LSE Research Online  nutritious food, modern housing, healthcare, education,
electricity, sanitation, appliances, computers, internet, and public transport. Their answer:
28–40 gigatons of materials per year, representing just 29–42% of current global
material use ResearchGate  (95 Gt in 2019). P2P Foundation  Energy requirements are
similarly modest. Even with a “fair inequality” allowance (40% above DLS), the world would
need only 175 EJ of energy and 39 Gt of materials — well within existing productive
capacity. P2P Foundation
The paper’s significance is in demonstrating that the problem is not insufficient global
production but its allocation. UAB Barcelona  uab  The world already produces enough to
meet everyone’s basic needs several times over. Standard GDP growth is inefficient
because capital flows toward profitable returns, not essential needs; prices of necessities

(food, housing) often rise faster than general prices; and Global South production is
structured toward exports rather than domestic provisioning. uab  In Hickel’s framing, the
ecological impossibility of 175x GDP growth is not merely an inconvenient constraint — it
reveals that the growth-first strategy was always the wrong approach. jasonhickel

The ecological math rules out growth-based solutions
The planetary boundaries dimension closes the case. Current global material extraction
stands at roughly 100 billion tonnes per year, against a sustainable limit estimated at
approximately 50 Gt/year (or 5–8 tonnes per capita). High-income countries consume 28
tonnes per capita — already 4x the sustainable level — while low-income countries use just
2 tonnes per capita. Wiley Online Library  Multiplying GDP by 5x with current material
coupling would push extraction to approximately 500 Gt/year — 10 times the sustainable
threshold. At 10x GDP, the figure reaches 1,000 Gt/year.
For carbon, the arithmetic is equally prohibitive. The remaining carbon budget for 1.5°C
warming (50% probability) stands at roughly 250–400 GtCO₂. Current emissions of ~37–
40 GtCO₂/year exhaust this in 6–10 years. A 5x GDP with current carbon intensity would
generate 185–200 GtCO₂/year, consuming the entire 2°C budget in about 6 years. Hickel
and Kallis’s widely cited 2020 analysis in New Political Economy showed that at 3% annual
GDP growth, meeting the 1.5°C target requires decarbonization at 10.5% per year —
against a historical best of 2–3%. Taylor & Francis Online  Taylor & Francis Online  Even the 11
high-income countries showing absolute CO₂-GDP decoupling (identified by Vogel and
Hickel in The Lancet Planetary Health, 2023) would take 220+ years at achieved rates to
reduce emissions by 95%, emitting 27 times their fair-share carbon budgets.

Conclusion
The research converges on three conclusions that should reshape development policy. First,
the growth-only path to eliminating poverty at meaningful thresholds is not merely slow but
physically impossible — requiring GDP multiplication that would breach every planetary
boundary within decades. Developmenteducation  Second, the inefficiency is not a bug of the
current system but a structural feature: when the poorest 60% receive only 5% of growth
income, no amount of aggregate expansion can substitute for distribution.
Giving What We Can +2  Third, the resources to end poverty already exist and are calculable:
the poverty gap at $6.85/day represents roughly 1.5–2.3% of global GDP, ScienceDirect
while the richest 1% alone captures income sufficient to close it several times over.
New Internationalist  The question is not whether humanity can afford to end poverty — Hickel
and Sullivan show that decent living for all requires less than a third of current resource
throughput UAB Barcelona  — but whether the political economy will permit the redistribution
that physics demands. ScienceDirect  Growth orthodoxy has treated this as a problem of
insufficient production. Worldeconomicsassociation  The evidence now shows it is a problem of
allocation operating under hard ecological constraints, Worldeconomicsassociation  and the
distinction matters for the survival of billions.
